# The Metric Trap: How Economic Indicators Became Elaborate Illusions

## A story about numbers that stopped meaning what we thought they meant

## **Opening: The Dilemma**

Consider a thought experiment: A central bank has adopted a single numerical target—2% inflation—as its primary metric for success. For twenty years, this works. The metric correlates with reality. But then something breaks.

A massive fiscal stimulus floods the economy with money. Inflation rises to 8.9%. The central bank begins raising rates to bring inflation back down. But as rates rise, the financial system begins to strain. Banks fail. Credit markets seize. The central bank faces a choice: continue raising rates to hit the 2% target, which would likely trigger a severe financial crisis, or pause rate increases, which would mean admitting that the target cannot be pursued without destroying the system itself.

What do they do?

They pause the rate increases. But they never admit that the target is unachievable. Instead, they begin to talk about different inflation measures. They point out that "core inflation"—inflation excluding food and energy—is closer to target. They discuss "sticky" inflation components. They suggest that some inflation is structural.

They begin to redefine success.

By early 2025, inflation is still 3.1-3.4%. By any honest measure, the central bank has failed to meet its target. And yet, rather than acknowledge this failure, the conversation shifts. The metric remains, but its meaning has changed.

---

## **Part One: The Target**

To understand why this moment mattered, you need to understand a particular kind of human tendency: our inclination to believe that if we can measure something precisely, we understand it.

The inflation target of 2% originated in a place of genuine insight. In the early 1990s, central bankers—scarred by the stagflation of the 1970s—decided they needed a clear, numerical objective. Not "low inflation." Not "price stability." A specific number: 2%.

It was elegant. It was defendable. It gave central banks a framework for decision-making and a way to communicate with the public. For nearly twenty years, it worked remarkably well.

Then, in 2020, something broke. And the way it broke would reveal something troubling about how we've organized our entire economic system around the pursuit of the wrong things.

The COVID pandemic unleashed an avalanche of government spending. In the United States alone, nearly $5 trillion flowed into the economy in stimulus. Central banks, in turn, kept interest rates near zero and purchased government bonds at a scale never before attempted.

By mid-2021, inflation began to rise. The Fed's official position was that this was "transitory"—a temporary blip caused by supply chain disruptions that would resolve itself. By mid-2022, inflation had reached 8.9%, the highest level in forty years. The Fed's position had shifted. Inflation wasn't transitory. It was real.

What followed was the most aggressive interest rate increase in Federal Reserve history. In the span of twelve months, the Fed raised rates from near zero to 4.5%. It was a shock to the system—the kind of shock that banks weren't prepared for.

In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank collapsed. In the weeks that followed, three other regional banks failed. By April, the crisis had spread to Europe. Credit markets seized. Investors demanded higher yields. The system was under strain.

And yet inflation was still above the 2% target.

Here's where the story gets interesting: The Fed's chair faced a choice. He could continue raising rates to bring inflation down to 2%, which would likely trigger a deeper financial crisis. Or he could hold steady, acknowledging that the inflation target couldn't be pursued without destroying the financial system itself.

He chose to hold steady.

But this created a peculiar situation. The Fed had spent years building its credibility on a single promise: we will keep inflation near 2%. Now it was clear that this promise couldn't be kept. Not because the Fed didn't want to keep it, but because the pursuit of it was creating other problems that were worse.

By January 2025, inflation was 3.1-3.4%. By most measures, the Fed had failed. And yet, rather than acknowledge this failure, something stranger happened: the conversation shifted.

Fed officials began talking about different inflation measures. They pointed out that core inflation—inflation excluding food and energy—was closer to the 2% target. They discussed "sticky" inflation components. They suggested that some inflation was structural and couldn't be reduced without causing harm.

In other words, they began to redefine success.

## **Part Two: The Incentive Trap**

Here's a thought experiment about how metrics become problems:

Imagine you're a CEO of a company. Your compensation is tied to earnings per share. Your board wants earnings per share to grow. The stock market rewards companies whose earnings per share grows. Investors pay premiums for earnings per share growth.

Now imagine that your company is mature. Real growth—new products, new markets, new capabilities—is slow and uncertain. It takes years. It's risky.

But you've discovered something: you can grow earnings per share without growing earnings.

Here's how: Your company has cash on its balance sheet. You use that cash to buy back your company's own stock. When you reduce the number of shares outstanding while keeping earnings constant, the earnings per share number goes up.

It's a purely financial maneuver. It creates no new economic value. But the stock market cares about earnings per share. So the stock price goes up. Your options become more valuable. Your compensation package balloons. You're rewarded.

This is not cheating. It's entirely legal. But it is, in a deeper sense, gaming the metric.

The question is: what happens when every CEO in America discovers this same insight?

By 2024, this is no longer a thought experiment. It's a documented pattern. Companies that couldn't grow had found a way to make it look like they were growing through financial engineering.

Think about what that comparison means. Over a fifteen-year period, American corporations spent roughly $4 trillion on buybacks while spending approximately $3-3.5 trillion on new capital investment—new factories, new equipment, new technology.

They spent comparable amounts removing shares from the market as they spent building the productive capacity of their companies.

This wouldn't matter if it were simply a financial game. But it had real consequences.

Research from McKinsey showed something unexpected: companies with the highest buyback rates had lower subsequent profitability. They had slower productivity growth. They were more vulnerable to disruption. In other words, the pursuit of the metric—earnings per share—was creating outcomes that were the opposite of what the metric was supposed to measure.

Earnings per share was supposed to reflect the health of the company. Instead, it was becoming a measure of financial engineering prowess.

A JPMorgan analyst created an index to measure what he called "profit quality"—the ratio of cash earnings to reported earnings. In 2015, for typical S&P 500 companies, this ratio was 0.94. That meant that for every dollar of reported earnings, there was 94 cents of actual cash. It was close to one-to-one.

By 2024, that ratio had fallen to 0.58. For every dollar of reported earnings, there was now only 58 cents of actual cash.

The companies were creating earnings the way a magician creates a coin from thin air. It looked real. The numbers looked real. But when you tried to grab hold of them, there was nothing there.

## **Part Three: The Measurement Problem**

Consider another thought experiment about how metrics can fail:

The government wants to measure unemployment. It needs a definition. The definition it chooses is: unemployed people actively seeking work, divided by the labor force.

This definition makes sense. It's measurable. It's simple. For decades, it works.

But then two things happen. First, the economy changes. A growing share of the workforce moves into gig work—work that's available through apps, that doesn't provide traditional employment status, that is unstable and often below workers' skill levels. These workers are "self-employed" and technically not "seeking work" when between gigs.

Second, the government expands disability insurance and makes it easier to qualify. Millions of working-age people who could potentially work move onto SSDI. They're no longer in the labor force.

So the headline unemployment rate—the number reported in newspapers, the number the Federal Reserve uses for policy decisions—becomes increasingly divorced from the actual state of joblessness. A person doing gig work below their skill level isn't counted as unemployed. A person on disability insurance isn't counted as unemployed. A person who has given up looking isn't counted as unemployed.

The metric remains the same. The definition remains the same. But what the metric measures has changed.

By early 2025, if you calculated unemployment using broader definitions—including gig workers, including discouraged workers, including people on disability who could work—you'd get a number roughly 3-4 percentage points higher than the headline figure.

But the headline figure is what gets reported. It's what people believe. It's what policy is built around.

There were actually multiple ways you could argue that the unemployment number was wrong.

The most straightforward was the measure called U-6, which includes not just unemployed workers, but also "underemployed" workers—people working part-time who want full-time work. The U-6 rate in January 2025 was 7.5%. That's substantially higher than the 4.1% headline rate. But you almost never see the U-6 reported in the newspapers.

Another issue was labor force participation. The labor force in 2015 included 62.7% of the adult population. By 2025, that had declined to 62.5%. Millions of people had left the labor force entirely. Some had retired early. Some had moved to Social Security Disability Insurance. Some had simply given up looking for work.

If you included those people in the unemployment calculation—if you asked, "what percentage of adults are unemployed or have left the labor force"—the number was substantially higher than 4.1%.

The Federal Reserve knew all this. It discussed these alternative measures in its internal communications. But when it came time to make policy decisions, it relied on the headline U-3 number. It was simpler. It was easier to communicate. And it told a story—the story of a tight labor market that was driving inflation higher.

But was the labor market really that tight?

In January 2025, there were roughly 9 million job openings in the United States and roughly 6.5 million unemployed people. On the surface, that looked like a mismatch where demand exceeded supply. But when you looked closer, the picture was different.

In tech, there were substantial numbers of job openings that required five or more years of AI experience—far more positions than there were qualified candidates available. The ratio of openings to qualified workers was sometimes measured in single digits. The "tight labor market" wasn't tight for those jobs. It was impossible.

In nursing, there were persistent shortages, with regions reporting unfilled positions for extended periods. The market was tight, but the tightness existed only in certain geographies. In rural areas and smaller cities, nursing positions went unfilled for months even as nursing programs reported increasing enrollments.

In manufacturing, precision machinists were scarce. So were specialized welders. But entry-level workers were abundant.

In other words, the tight labor market was a fiction created by aggregating different markets. If you looked at the headline number, the labor market was tight. If you looked at the actual distribution of supply and demand, the picture was more complex. There was genuine scarcity in some areas and genuine surplus in others.

But the Federal Reserve was making policy decisions based on the aggregated number.

By January 2025, wage growth—another metric the Fed was watching—was officially 3.8-4.2%. This seemed to confirm the tight labor market thesis. If unemployment was low and wages were rising, it stood to reason that workers had leverage.

But consider the mechanics: A gig worker earning $18 an hour is counted in those wage statistics. When the government averages her gig wages in with the wages of full-time workers earning $80,000 a year, her low wage affects the average. If millions of people move from traditional employment to gig work, the average wage figure can rise even if no individual worker actually sees a raise.

But there was another, stranger statistical effect at play: compositional shifts.

As the labor market had tightened, low-wage workers had been the first to leave. They'd taken early retirement. They'd moved to disability insurance. They'd given up looking. This meant that the workers who remained in the labor force were, on average, higher-wage workers.

So even if wages for an individual worker hadn't increased, the average wage could increase, simply because the lower-wage workers had left the pool.

When you adjusted for this compositional effect, actual wage growth—the increase in wages for the same worker over time—was closer to 2-2.5%. That was much closer to inflation, which meant that real wages—the actual purchasing power workers had—were essentially flat.

Workers felt poorer even though the statistics said they were earning more.

## **Part Four: The Bridge**

Here's a question that doesn't get asked much: what do inflation targeting, earnings per share, and unemployment statistics have in common?

The answer is that they are all metrics that were designed to measure something real but have become divorced from what they were supposed to measure.

Inflation targeting was designed to keep prices stable. But inflation, it turned out, was not a single phenomenon. There was housing inflation, which hit renters particularly hard. There was healthcare inflation. There was education inflation. There was food inflation. When you created a single metric—the Consumer Price Index—you had to make choices about how to weight these different components.

The CPI weighted housing at about 6% of the basket. But for a renter in 2025, housing made up 28-35% of their budget. So if housing inflation was 7-9% and the CPI said inflation was 3%, the CPI was failing to measure the inflation that mattered most to that renter.

Earnings per share was designed to measure how profitable a company was. But profitability could be achieved in different ways. It could come from genuine productivity improvements. Or it could come from financial engineering—buybacks, restructuring, accounting changes. The metric didn't distinguish between the two.

Unemployment was designed to measure how many people couldn't find work. But it didn't account for people who had given up looking. It didn't account for people working gig jobs below their skill level. It didn't account for the composition of the unemployed—whether they were long-term unemployed or recently unemployed, whether they had skills that were in demand or skills that had become obsolete.

In each case, the metric had been designed as a simple summary of something complex. And in each case, the metric had become the target.

There's a phenomenon in social science called Goodhart's Law. It goes like this: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

The idea is that once you decide to optimize for something, people figure out how to game it. They find workarounds. They find ways to hit the target without actually achieving what the target was supposed to represent.

The Fed had optimized for the 2% inflation target. Companies had optimized for earnings per share. The government had optimized for low unemployment. And in each case, the pursuit of the metric had led to outcomes that were the opposite of what the metric was supposed to achieve.

The Fed's pursuit of the inflation target had created financial fragility—banks weren't hedged against the rapid rate increases required to hit the target.

Companies' pursuit of earnings per share had created productive stagnation—they were buying back stock instead of investing in research and development.

The government's pursuit of low unemployment had created statistical fiction—people were moving into gig work and disability insurance to keep the headline number low.

And here's the thing that kept me up at night when I was researching this: nobody was doing this consciously. Nobody woke up and said, "I'm going to game the system." The Fed genuinely believed that keeping inflation near 2% was the right policy. Companies genuinely believed that returning capital to shareholders via buybacks was good management. The government genuinely believed that its employment statistics were accurate.

They were all optimizing sincerely. They were all pursuing metrics that made sense in theory. But the system had developed a kind of perverse intelligence—it had learned to create the appearance of success while failure accumulated unseen.

## **Part Five: The Unraveling**

By early 2025, if you were paying attention, you could see the cracks forming. The system hadn't broken yet, but it was breaking.

In the housing market, mortgage rates had risen from 3% in 2021 to 6.8-7.2% by early 2025. This meant that the same house that required a $1,200 monthly payment in 2021 now required an $1,900 monthly payment. Affordability had collapsed. Home sales had dropped 35% from the peak. Builders had stopped building. The housing market was seizing up.

But the inflation target was still not being met. The Fed was supposedly tightening policy to bring inflation down to 2%, but inflation remained at 3.1-3.4%. Something wasn't working.

In corporate earnings, the pattern had become obvious to anyone watching closely: companies were growing earnings per share without growing actual earnings. The profit quality index had fallen to 0.58. Companies were borrowing money to buy back stock. The amount of debt in the corporate sector was at historic highs. And it was all in service of hitting the earnings per share target.

But it was becoming harder to hit. By late 2024, companies had started warning that earnings growth would slow in 2025. Some of this was real—sales growth was slowing. But some of it was the reversal of financial engineering. When you've already bought back stock, you can't buy back as much next year. You have to actually grow earnings.

In the labor market, the cracks were appearing in a different way. Despite low unemployment, worker productivity was not accelerating. Real wage growth—adjusted for inflation—was essentially flat. And yet companies were struggling to find workers, especially in skilled positions.

The labor market wasn't tight in the way the headline number suggested. It was fragmented. It was mismatched. And the more the Fed tightened policy to fight inflation, the more pressure it put on companies to cut costs, which meant cutting workers, which threatened to break the unemployment statistics' credibility entirely.

By January 2025, something strange was happening: the Fed was holding interest rates steady—not raising them further—even though inflation was still above the 2% target. Fed officials explained this by pointing to "financial stability concerns." They were worried that if they raised rates further, the financial system would seize up again.

In other words, they were admitting that the pursuit of the inflation target was incompatible with financial stability.

And that admission—made quietly, in policy statements and Fed communications, but never explicitly stated—was an admission that the system was broken.

## **Part Six: The Pattern**

I spent a lot of time trying to understand how this happened. How had the world's most sophisticated central banks, the world's most advanced corporations, the world's largest economy, all arrived at a place where the metrics they were optimizing for had become elaborate fictions?

The answer, I think, involves recognizing a pattern that shows up again and again.

When you create a metric to measure something complex, you are necessarily simplifying. You are making trade-offs. You are deciding what to include and what to exclude.

For long periods of time, this simplification works. The metric correlates with the thing you're trying to measure. Inflation targeting correlates with price stability. Earnings per share correlates with company profitability. Unemployment rate correlates with labor market slack.

But then, gradually, the incentive structure around the metric creates a force. People and organizations begin to optimize for the metric itself rather than for the underlying reality it was supposed to represent.

Once this happens, there's a period where the metric continues to look good even as the underlying reality deteriorates. This is the deceptive period, when the system looks like it's working even though it's breaking.

During this period, anyone who points out the gap between the metric and reality is ignored. They're dismissed as not understanding the system. They're told that the metric is actually correct and their intuition is wrong.

But eventually—and this always eventually—the gap becomes too large to ignore. The metric and reality have diverged so far that it becomes impossible to maintain the pretense that the metric is meaningful.

When that moment comes, there's usually a crisis. The system adjusts. A new metric is adopted. A new equilibrium is found.

We are now in the late stages of this pattern. We're in the period where the metrics look good and the reality is breaking. We're in the period where central bankers are acknowledging, in private, that the system doesn't work, but continuing to pretend, in public, that it does.

It won't last. These things never do.

What comes next is uncertain. It could be a gentle transition to new frameworks. It could be a crisis that forces the transition. But transition will come.

And when it does, we'll have to confront a troubling question: what else are we measuring wrong?

## **Epilogue: The Institutional Trap**

Here's a final thought experiment about why systems persist even when they've stopped working:

Imagine you're a central bank leader. You've built your institutional credibility on a single promise: we will keep inflation near 2%. You've spent decades refining this framework. You've built an entire bureaucracy around it. Your reputation is tied to it.

Then you discover that the framework doesn't work. The pursuit of the 2% target is creating financial instability that is worse than the inflation it's trying to prevent.

You have a choice. You can acknowledge this publicly. You can say: "We've discovered that this framework is obsolete. We need to build a new one from scratch."

Or you can do what most institutions do when their foundational metrics have become problematic: you can adjust how you report the metric. You can talk about different inflation measures. You can discuss why the metric is less important than you previously claimed. You can redefine what success means.

The second path preserves your institutional credibility in the short term. It avoids admitting that you were wrong. It allows you to claim that you're still winning, just by a different definition of victory.

But it also locks your institution into a framework that doesn't work. It makes it harder to adapt when reality finally forces a change.

What happens when every major institution in the economy is simultaneously optimizing for metrics that have become divorced from reality, and simultaneously refusing to acknowledge this fact?

That's the question that matters.

Because the answer is: the system becomes fragile in ways that are hard to see until the moment it breaks. And in our interconnected economy, when one system breaks, all of them do.
